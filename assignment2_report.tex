\section{Q1. Uniform Quantizer Design for 5G OFDM Signals}

The OFDM sample amplitude is modeled as a Gaussian random variable:
\begin{equation*}
X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2), \qquad
f_X(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}.
\end{equation*}

For an 8-bit uniform quantizer with clipping at $\pm V_{\max}$ and loading factor
\begin{equation*}
\gamma = \frac{V_{\max}}{\sigma},
\end{equation*}
we balance two effects:
\begin{itemize}
\item[$\rightarrow$] granular noise (inside clipping range),
\item[$\rightarrow$] overload noise (tails beyond clipping range).
\end{itemize}

Intuition: the OFDM waveform is a sum of many independently modulated subcarriers. By central-limit behavior, the time-domain sample histogram becomes close to Gaussian. This means low-amplitude samples are very common, while large peaks are rare but important. If the quantizer range is too narrow, rare peaks are clipped hard and create large error energy. If range is too wide, clipping reduces but quantization steps become coarse for the majority of samples near zero. Therefore, the best design must trade these two error mechanisms.

\subsection*{Q1.1 Derivation of Granular and Overload Noise Power}

The OFDM signal amplitude is modeled as a Gaussian random variable 
$X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ with PDF:
\begin{equation}
    f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}\,e^{-x^2/2\sigma^2}
\end{equation}

For a uniform mid-tread quantizer with $B = 8$ bits, clipping threshold 
$\pm V_{\max}$, and loading factor $\gamma = V_{\max}/\sigma$, the step 
size is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta = \frac{2V_{\max}}{2^B} = \frac{2V_{\max}}{2^8} = \frac{2\gamma\sigma}{256}
\end{equation}

The total quantization noise power is:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,tot} = P_{q,gran} + P_{q,ol}
\end{equation}

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{Overload Noise Power $P_{q,ol}$}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

When the input exceeds $\pm V_{\max}$, the quantizer clips the output 
to $\pm V_{\max}$. The resulting error is $(x - V_{\max})$ for 
$x > V_{\max}$ and $(x + V_{\max})$ for $x < -V_{\max}$. The overload 
noise power is therefore:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,ol} = \int_{V_{\max}}^{\infty} (x - V_{\max})^2\,f_X(x)\,dx
             + \int_{-\infty}^{-V_{\max}} (x + V_{\max})^2\,f_X(x)\,dx
\end{equation}

By symmetry of $f_X(x)$ about zero, both integrals are equal:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,ol} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}
               \int_{V_{\max}}^{\infty} (x - V_{\max})^2\,
               e^{-x^2/2\sigma^2}\,dx
\end{equation}

Substituting $u = x/\sigma$, so $x = \sigma u$, $dx = \sigma\,du$, 
and noting that $V_{\max} = \gamma\sigma$:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,ol} = \frac{2\sigma^2}{\sqrt{2\pi}}
               \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} (u - \gamma)^2\,
               e^{-u^2/2}\,du
\end{equation}

Expanding $(u-\gamma)^2 = u^2 - 2\gamma u + \gamma^2$ and splitting 
into three integrals:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,ol} = 2\sigma^2 \left[
        \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} u^2\,\frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,du
        \;-\; 2\gamma \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} u\,\frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,du
        \;+\; \gamma^2 \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} \frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,du
    \right]
\end{equation}

The third integral is directly the Q-function by definition:
\begin{equation}
    \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} \frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,du = Q(\gamma)
\end{equation}

For the second integral, we use $u\,e^{-u^2/2} = 
-\dfrac{d}{du}\!\left(e^{-u^2/2}\right)$:
\begin{equation}
    \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} u\,\frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,du
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\Bigl[-e^{-u^2/2}\Bigr]_{\gamma}^{\infty}
    = \frac{e^{-\gamma^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}
    = \phi(\gamma)
\end{equation}

For the first integral, we apply integration by parts with 
$p = u$ and $dq = u\,e^{-u^2/2}\,du$, giving $dp = du$ and 
$q = -e^{-u^2/2}$:
\begin{align}
    \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} u^2\,\frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,du
    &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left(
        \Bigl[-u\,e^{-u^2/2}\Bigr]_{\gamma}^{\infty}
        + \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} e^{-u^2/2}\,du
       \right) \nonumber\\
    &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left(
        \gamma\,e^{-\gamma^2/2}
        + \sqrt{2\pi}\,Q(\gamma)
       \right) \nonumber\\
    &= \gamma\,\phi(\gamma) + Q(\gamma)
\end{align}

Substituting all three results back:
\begin{align}
    P_{q,ol} &= 2\sigma^2\Bigl[
                 \gamma\phi(\gamma) + Q(\gamma)
                 - 2\gamma\phi(\gamma)
                 + \gamma^2 Q(\gamma)
                \Bigr] \nonumber\\
             &= 2\sigma^2\Bigl[
                 (1+\gamma^2)\,Q(\gamma) - \gamma\,\phi(\gamma)
                \Bigr]
\end{align}

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
    P_{q,ol} = 2\sigma^2\Bigl[(1+\gamma^2)\,Q(\gamma) 
               - \gamma\,\phi(\gamma)\Bigr]
}
\end{equation}

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{Granular Noise Power $P_{q,gran}$}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

The granular noise arises from rounding within the quantizer's operating 
range $[-V_{\max}, +V_{\max}]$. For a quantizer with decision boundaries 
$\{b_k\}$ and reproduction levels $\{q_k\}$, the exact granular distortion is:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,gran} = \sum_{k} \int_{b_k}^{b_{k+1}} 
                 (x - q_k)^2\,f_X(x)\,dx
\end{equation}

This expression weights the squared quantization error in each cell by 
the probability of the input falling in that cell, summed over all 
$2^B = 256$ quantization intervals. For the $k$-th cell centred at 
$q_k$, the boundaries are $b_k = q_k - \Delta/2$ and 
$b_{k+1} = q_k + \Delta/2$. Expanding $(x - q_k)^2$ and using the 
fact that $f_X(x)$ is approximately constant over one step $\Delta$ 
when $\Delta \ll \sigma$ (high-resolution approximation):
\begin{align}
    \int_{b_k}^{b_{k+1}} (x-q_k)^2\,f_X(x)\,dx 
    &\approx f_X(q_k)\int_{-\Delta/2}^{\Delta/2} \epsilon^2\,d\epsilon 
     \nonumber\\
    &= f_X(q_k)\cdot\frac{\Delta^3}{12}
\end{align}

Summing over all cells inside $[-V_{\max}, +V_{\max}]$ and replacing 
$\sum_k f_X(q_k)\,\Delta \approx \Pr(|X| \leq V_{\max}) 
= 1 - 2Q(\gamma)$:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,gran} \approx \frac{\Delta^2}{12}\,\bigl(1 - 2Q(\gamma)\bigr)
\end{equation}

Substituting $\Delta = \dfrac{2\gamma\sigma}{2^B}$:
\begin{equation}
    P_{q,gran} \approx \frac{1}{12}\left(\frac{2\gamma\sigma}{2^B}
                \right)^2 \bigl(1 - 2Q(\gamma)\bigr)
                = \frac{\gamma^2\sigma^2}{3 \cdot 2^{2B}}
                \bigl(1 - 2Q(\gamma)\bigr)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
    P_{q,gran} = \frac{\gamma^2\sigma^2}{3 \cdot 2^{2B}}
                 \bigl(1 - 2Q(\gamma)\bigr)
}
\end{equation}
%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{Total Quantization Noise and SQNR}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

Combining both contributions:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
    P_{q,tot} = \underbrace{\frac{\gamma^2\sigma^2}{3 \cdot 2^{2B}}
                \bigl(1-2Q(\gamma)\bigr)}_{\text{granular noise}}
                +\;
                \underbrace{2\sigma^2\bigl[(1+\gamma^2)Q(\gamma) 
                - \gamma\phi(\gamma)\bigr]}_{\text{overload noise}}
}
\end{equation}

The Signal-to-Quantization-Noise Ratio is then:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SQNR}(\gamma) = 10\log_{10}\!\left(
        \frac{\sigma^2}{P_{q,tot}(\gamma)}
    \right) \quad \text{(dB)}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{align*}
    \phi(\gamma) &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-\gamma^2/2} 
                 &&\text{(standard normal PDF at } \gamma\text{)}\\
    Q(\gamma)    &= \int_{\gamma}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,
                    e^{-u^2/2}\,du 
                 &&\text{(Q-function / Gaussian tail probability)}\\
    \gamma       &= \frac{V_{\max}}{\sigma} 
                 &&\text{(loading factor)}\\
    B            &= 8 
                 &&\text{(quantizer resolution in bits)}
\end{align*}

\subsection*{Q1.2 Numerical Sweep and Optimal Loading Factor}

We sweep the loading factor $\gamma = V_{\max}/\sigma$ over the range 
$\gamma \in [0.1,\, 7]$ in steps of $0.01$, which covers the physically 
relevant region for an 8-bit Gaussian quantizer: below $\gamma = 0.1$ the 
quantizer is severely overloaded, and beyond $\gamma = 7$ the Gaussian tail 
probability $Q(\gamma)$ is negligibly small so overload noise is essentially 
zero while granular noise dominates completely.

For each $\gamma$, the theoretical noise powers derived in Q1.1 are evaluated 
numerically:
\begin{align}
    P_{q,gran}(\gamma) &= \frac{\gamma^2 \sigma^2}{3 \cdot 2^{2B}}
                          \bigl(1 - 2Q(\gamma)\bigr) \\[6pt]
    P_{q,ol}(\gamma)   &= 2\sigma^2\bigl[(1+\gamma^2)\,Q(\gamma) 
                          - \gamma\,\phi(\gamma)\bigr] \\[6pt]
    P_{q,tot}(\gamma)  &= P_{q,gran}(\gamma) + P_{q,ol}(\gamma)
\end{align}
with $B = 8$, $\sigma = 1$ (normalised), 
$\phi(\gamma) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\gamma^2/2}$, and 
$Q(\gamma) = \frac{1}{2}\operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\gamma/\sqrt{2}\right)$.

The optimal loading factor is identified as the $\gamma$ that minimises the 
total noise power:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_{\text{opt}} = \arg\min_{\gamma}\; P_{q,tot}(\gamma).
\end{equation}

From the numerical sweep:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_{\text{opt}} \approx 3.926, \qquad 
    \text{SQNR}_{\text{opt}} \approx 40.57 \text{ dB}.
\end{equation}

This result is notably lower than the classical $6.02B + 1.76 \approx 49.9$ 
dB formula for a full-scale sinusoidal input. The difference arises because 
the Gaussian distribution places significant probability mass in the tails, 
increasing average distortion even at the optimal clipping level. The 
sinusoidal formula assumes a perfectly bounded, uniformly distributed 
amplitude, which is not the case here.

Figure~\ref{fig:noise_components} shows the three noise curves on a 
log-scale. The crossover point where $P_{q,gran} = P_{q,ol}$ is close to but 
not exactly at $\gamma_{\text{opt}}$: the true minimum of $P_{q,tot}$ depends 
on the \emph{rates of change} of both curves, not merely their equality. The 
SQNR curve in Figure~\ref{fig:sqnr_theory} is visibly asymmetric about the 
peak: it drops sharply for $\gamma < \gamma_{\text{opt}}$ because overload 
noise grows rapidly as Gaussian tails are clipped hard, while for 
$\gamma > \gamma_{\text{opt}}$ granular noise grows only as $\gamma^2$, so 
the penalty for over-ranging is much milder than for under-ranging.

\vspace{0.5cm}
Design-logic interpretation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item For $\gamma < \gamma_{\text{opt}}$: overload dominates and SQNR 
          drops sharply because the Gaussian tails are heavily clipped.
    \item Around $\gamma_{\text{opt}}$: overload and granular distortions 
          are balanced at their optimal tradeoff point.
    \item For $\gamma > \gamma_{\text{opt}}$: clipping is negligible but 
          coarse quantization dominates and SQNR degrades slowly.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Q1.3 Empirical SQNR with OFDM Dataset}

For the measured OFDM sequence, the empirical SQNR at each loading factor 
$\gamma$ is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SQNR}_{\text{emp}}(\gamma) = 10\log_{10}\!\left(
    \frac{\mathbb{E}[x^2]}{\mathbb{E}[(x-\hat{x})^2]}\right)
\end{equation}
where $x$ are the original unquantized samples and $\hat{x}$ are their 
quantized counterparts obtained by passing $x$ through an 8-bit uniform 
mid-tread quantizer with clipping threshold $V_{\max} = \gamma\,\sigma_{\text{emp}}$.

\subsubsection*{Procedure}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Load the complex baseband samples from \texttt{ofdm\_dataset.mat} 
          and center the data by subtracting the sample mean.
    \item Estimate the empirical standard deviation $\sigma_{\text{emp}}$ 
          from the centered samples.
    \item Sweep $\gamma$ over the same range as Q1.2, setting 
          $V_{\max} = \gamma\,\sigma_{\text{emp}}$ at each step.
    \item Quantize the samples using an 8-bit uniform mid-tread quantizer 
          with step size $\Delta = 2V_{\max}/2^B$.
    \item Compute $\text{SQNR}_{\text{emp}}(\gamma)$ at each step and 
          overlay with the theoretical curve from Q1.2.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Numerical Results}

From the provided \texttt{ofdm\_dataset.mat}:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{\text{emp}} \approx 0.9999995, \qquad
    \gamma_{\text{opt,emp}} \approx 3.887,  \qquad
    \text{SQNR}_{\text{opt,emp}} \approx 40.61\ \text{dB}.
\end{equation}

The shift in optimal loading factor relative to the theoretical prediction is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\gamma = \gamma_{\text{opt,emp}} - \gamma_{\text{opt,theory}} 
    \approx 3.887 - 3.926 = -0.039.
\end{equation}

The empirical optimal shifts slightly left (toward a narrower clipping range) 
compared to theory. This means the real OFDM signal has marginally lighter 
tails than a perfect Gaussian, so less clipping range is needed to achieve 
the best SQNR. This is physically consistent: practical OFDM waveforms use 
windowing and guard intervals that slightly reduce the probability of extreme 
amplitude peaks relative to the pure i.i.d. Gaussian model.

\subsubsection*{Interpretation}

The close agreement between the two curves (peak difference $< 0.1$ dB, 
$\Delta\gamma \approx -0.039$) confirms that the Gaussian i.i.d. amplitude 
model is an excellent approximation for this OFDM dataset. Any residual 
discrepancy has three physical origins:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Finite sample effects:} The empirical histogram is 
          estimated from a finite number of samples, so the tails of the 
          distribution are not perfectly resolved. This introduces small 
          statistical fluctuations in $\text{SQNR}_{\text{emp}}$.

    \item \textbf{Pilot subcarriers and guard bands:} Practical 5G OFDM 
          frames contain pilot tones and null subcarriers that do not carry 
          data. These reduce the effective number of independently modulated 
          subcarriers, slightly weakening the central-limit argument and 
          causing minor departures from Gaussianity.

    \item \textbf{Windowing and cyclic prefix:} Time-domain windowing 
          applied at the transmitter to control spectral leakage modifies the 
          amplitude envelope of the OFDM symbol, reducing the probability of 
          large peaks (lower PAPR) and therefore shifting the empirical 
          optimal $\gamma$ slightly below the theoretical value.
\end{itemize}

Overall, the gap between curves acts as a diagnostic rather than an error: 
its smallness here validates the Gaussian design assumption, while a large 
gap would have indicated a fundamental modelling mismatch requiring a 
non-Gaussian quantizer design


\section{Q2. Radar Detection in Colored Noise}

Given
\begin{equation*}
r[n] = s[n-n_0] + v[n],
\end{equation*}
where clutter is AR(1):
\begin{equation*}
v[n] = \alpha v[n-1] + w[n], \qquad w[n]\sim\mathcal{CN}(0,\sigma_w^2).
\end{equation*}

Intuition: the target echo is a delayed copy of known sequence $s[n]$, so correlation with $s[n]$ is the natural detector. However, clutter is not white; it has memory (strong low-frequency behavior). In such a case, naive matched filtering is no longer statistically optimal. Pre-whitening first removes clutter correlation so the subsequent correlation stage operates in a near-white-noise setting.

Hence, this problem is not only about implementing correlation, but about correcting the noise statistics first so that matched-filter optimality assumptions are restored.

\subsection*{Q2.1 Matched Filtering and Cross-Correlation Equivalence}
For a complex pulse, matched filter is
\begin{equation*}
h(t)=s^*(T-t).
\end{equation*}
Filter output:
\begin{equation*}
y(t)=\int r(\tau)h(t-\tau)\,d\tau
=\int r(\tau)s^*(T-(t-\tau))\,d\tau.
\end{equation*}
At $t=T$:
\begin{equation*}
y(T)=\int r(\tau)s^*(\tau)\,d\tau = R_{rs}(0).
\end{equation*}
Hence, matched-filter sampling at $T$ is equivalent to zero-lag cross-correlation.

This equivalence is the key reason cross-correlation is used directly in discrete implementations: it is exactly the matched-filter operation under standard conventions.

Equivalent discrete-time statement used in code:
\begin{equation*}
y[m] = \sum_n r[n]s^*[n-m] = (r \star s)[m].
\end{equation*}
Therefore the detection statistic is the cross-correlation peak over lag index $m$.

\subsection*{Q2.2 Estimation of $\alpha$ from Clutter-Only Samples}

From first 500 clutter-only samples,
\begin{equation*}
\hat{\alpha}=\arg\min_{\alpha}\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}|v[n]-\alpha v[n-1]|^2
=\frac{\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}v[n]v^*[n-1]}{\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}|v[n-1]|^2}.
\end{equation*}

This estimator is the complex least-squares/Yule-Walker form for AR(1), obtained by orthogonality of the residual with the regressor $v[n-1]$.

Detailed derivation idea:
\begin{itemize}
\item[$\rightarrow$] define cost $J(\alpha)=\sum |v[n]-\alpha v[n-1]|^2$,
\item[$\rightarrow$] take Wirtinger derivative with respect to $\alpha^*$,
\item[$\rightarrow$] set derivative to zero to obtain normal equation,
\item[$\rightarrow$] solve for ratio of lag-1 cross-term to lag-0 energy term.
\end{itemize}

Connection to Yule-Walker form:
\begin{equation*}
R_v[1] \approx \alpha R_v[0]
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\hat{\alpha} \approx \frac{\hat{R}_v[1]}{\hat{R}_v[0]}.
\end{equation*}
This gives the same estimator and makes the statistical interpretation explicit.

\subsection*{Q2.3 FIR Pre-whitening Filter}

Using $v[n]-\alpha v[n-1]=w[n]$, choose
\begin{equation*}
H_w(z)=1-\hat{\alpha}z^{-1},
\end{equation*}
with impulse response
\begin{equation*}
h_w[n]=\delta[n]-\hat{\alpha}\delta[n-1].
\end{equation*}

This filter cancels the AR(1) memory term and leaves approximately white innovation noise, making matched filtering closer to the optimal AWGN condition.

Frequency-domain view: AR(1) clutter has PSD proportional to $1/|1-\alpha e^{-j\omega}|^2$. Multiplying by $|1-\hat{\alpha}e^{-j\omega}|^2$ flattens this spectrum when $\hat{\alpha}\approx\alpha$.

Therefore the whitened receiver effectively equalizes the clutter color before matched filtering, preventing low-frequency clutter dominance in the correlation output.

\subsection*{Q2.4 Receiver Architectures}
\begin{itemize}
\item[$\rightarrow$] Receiver A (Naive): cross-correlate raw $r[n]$ with raw $s[n]$.
\item[$\rightarrow$] Receiver B (Whitened): pre-whiten both $r[n]$ and $s[n]$, then cross-correlate.
\end{itemize}

Receiver B is expected to improve peak prominence and output SNR because strong low-frequency clutter energy is flattened before correlation.

Engineering expectation in plots:
\begin{itemize}
\item[$\rightarrow$] Receiver A: elevated background floor and weaker peak-to-floor contrast,
\item[$\rightarrow$] Receiver B: cleaner floor and sharper dominant peak near true delay.
\end{itemize}

Output SNR metric:
\begin{equation*}
\text{SNR}_{\text{out}} = 10\log_{10}
\left(\frac{|C[k_{\text{peak}}]|^2}{\mathbb{E}\{|C[k]|^2\,\text{outside}\,[k_{\text{peak}}-50,k_{\text{peak}}+50]\}}\right).
\end{equation*}

In implementation, the noise floor is estimated from all lags excluding a protection window around the peak ($\pm 50$ samples), so peak sidelobes do not bias the variance estimate.

\section{Q3. Raised Cosine (RC) Filter}

In practical digital communication systems, pulse shaping is not only about bandwidth control, but also about controlling Inter-Symbol Interference (ISI). The raised-cosine (RC) spectrum is specifically designed so that, after matched filtering and symbol-rate sampling, neighboring symbols do not interfere. This is why RC filters are fundamental in modem design.

Given roll-off factor $\beta$ and symbol period $T$, the RC frequency response is
\begin{equation*}
H_{RC}(f)=
\begin{cases}
T, & |f|\le\dfrac{1-\beta}{2T},\\[4pt]
\dfrac{T}{2}\left[1+\cos\left(\dfrac{\pi T}{\beta}\left(|f|-\dfrac{1-\beta}{2T}\right)\right)\right], & \dfrac{1-\beta}{2T}<|f|\le\dfrac{1+\beta}{2T},\\[6pt]
0, & |f|>\dfrac{1+\beta}{2T}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Since $H_{RC}(f)$ is real and even, its impulse response is also real and can be obtained using
\begin{equation*}
h_{RC}(t)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}H_{RC}(f)e^{j2\pi ft}\,df
=2\int_0^{\infty}H_{RC}(f)\cos(2\pi ft)\,df.
\end{equation*}

To evaluate this integral, split the spectrum into two parts:
\begin{itemize}
\item[$\rightarrow$] flat passband contribution for $0\le f\le \dfrac{1-\beta}{2T}$,
\item[$\rightarrow$] cosine roll-off contribution for $\dfrac{1-\beta}{2T}<f\le \dfrac{1+\beta}{2T}$.
\end{itemize}

The passband integral gives the familiar sinc-type term, while the roll-off integral contributes a cosine-modulated correction that introduces the denominator $1-(2\beta t/T)^2$. After simplification, the closed-form RC impulse response is
\begin{equation*}
h_{RC}(t)=\frac{\sin(\pi t/T)}{\pi t/T}\cdot\frac{\cos(\pi\beta t/T)}{1-(2\beta t/T)^2}.
\end{equation*}

This is the standard result used in communication texts and software libraries.

\subsection*{Special Points and Continuity}
The formula above has removable singularities at specific time instants. Therefore we evaluate limits to obtain finite values:
\begin{equation*}
h_{RC}(0)=1,
\end{equation*}
and for $\beta\neq 0$,
\begin{equation*}
h_{RC}\left(\pm\frac{T}{2\beta}\right)=\frac{\beta}{2}\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2\beta}\right).
\end{equation*}

These values are important in implementation because direct substitution in code can produce a division-by-zero warning if limits are not handled explicitly.

\subsection*{Nyquist ISI Interpretation}
The RC pulse satisfies the Nyquist zero-ISI condition:
\begin{equation*}
h_{RC}(nT)=
\begin{cases}
1, & n=0,\\
0, & n\in\mathbb{Z},\;n\neq 0.
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}
Hence, at ideal symbol-rate sampling instants, each symbol sees only its own pulse and no deterministic interference from neighboring symbols.

\subsection*{Role of Roll-Off Factor $\beta$}
The roll-off factor controls the bandwidth-time tradeoff:
\begin{itemize}
\item[$\rightarrow$] occupied (one-sided) baseband bandwidth is $\dfrac{1+\beta}{2T}$,
\item[$\rightarrow$] smaller $\beta$ gives tighter spectrum but longer time-domain ringing,
\item[$\rightarrow$] larger $\beta$ gives wider spectrum but faster decay in time.
\end{itemize}

So, $\beta$ is a system-level design knob: spectral efficiency pushes toward smaller $\beta$, while timing robustness and practical filter truncation push toward larger $\beta$.

Extreme cases:
\begin{itemize}
\item[$\rightarrow$] $\beta \to 0$: RC tends to the ideal sinc pulse (minimum bandwidth, maximum time spread),
\item[$\rightarrow$] $\beta \to 1$: widest transition band, smoother spectral edges, and better time localization.
\end{itemize}
